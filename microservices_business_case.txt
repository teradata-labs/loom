The business case for microservices isn't about the first six months; it's about the first six years. To call it an 'engineering ego trip' ignores the catastrophic cost of the 'Monolithic Stalemate.'

1. The 'Fast Now' Fallacy:
Yes, a monolith is faster for the first few features. But speed is a vector, not a scalar. Once your 'Modular Monolith' reaches a certain size, the 'Coordination Tax' becomes a permanent drag on the business. Every release becomes a negotiation between teams. Microservices allow a business to parallelize its bets. While Team A is experimenting with a new checkout flow, Team B is rewriting the search algorithm. In a monolith, they are constantly stepping on each other's toes.

2. Resilience is a Business Requirement, Not an Engineering Perk:
When a monolith goes down, the business stops making money. Period. In 2026, a 99.9% uptime is no longer enough for competitive markets. The 'partial failure' states that the Monolith Expert fears are actually our greatest strength. We can lose the recommendation engine, the loyalty points service, and the email notifier, and the customer can still click 'Buy.' That is not an ego trip; that is risk management.

3. The 'Data Consistency' Bogeyman:
Monolith advocates treat ACID transactions like a magic wand. But in a global business, you can't have a single database in one region anyway. You are forced into distributed systems the moment you scale globally. Microservices simply force you to face that reality early and build a system that is robust against the physics of the internet.

We don't build microservices to feel smart. We build them so that when the business needs to pivot next Tuesday, we don't have to tell the CEO 'That will take six months of refactoring the core monolith.'
